Precious metals are at historic highs. Gold surpassed US$4,000 per ounce for the first time in 2025, a 66% annual gain — its strongest performance since 1979 — and J.P. Morgan projects prices toward US$5,000/oz by Q4 2026. Silver hit multi-decade highs above US$60/oz. In this environment, every hour a grinding mill is offline represents lost ounces at prices that make each unplanned downtime event dramatically more expensive than at any prior point in mining history.

Grinding mills are the productive core of mineral processing operations in Canada, and their large open gear drives rank among the most failure-prone, highest-consequence components on any mine site. At current gold prices, a single unplanned SAG or ball mill shutdown at a major Canadian gold mine can cost CAD $500,000–$1,500,000 per day in lost production value alone, before emergency repair costs, replacement parts, and mobilization premiums. For a precious metals operation running at full capacity in a record-price environment, gear failure is not just a maintenance problem — it is a revenue crisis.

The solution is early detection, deployed frequently and quickly without extended mill downtime. That technology is Eddy Current Array (ECA) inspection through grease, and the BU - Services is already delivering it. ECA through-grease gear inspections are live and operational across 12 active accounts spanning Canadian gold, silver, iron ore, oil sands, and cement operations, plus an international program in Suriname — with proven results across a diverse range of mill types and operating environments. This is not a new concept being introduced to the market; it is a validated, in-service inspection program being strategically scaled to reach every major mining operator in Canada.

Powered by the SG NDT Micro S2G2 ECA instrument and EMMA software for gear inspection, and advanced robotic ultrasonic testing (UT) systems for mill shell, trunnion, and head thickness measurement, — manufactured by a fellow Ekoscan Integrity Group company in Lévis, Québec — and delivered by the BU - Services team of Nucleom Inc. and Nortech Advanced NDT operating across seven Canadian offices, this commercial plan defines the strategy, targets, and revenue roadmap to grow this service from its current customer base to full-scale penetration of the Canadian mining sector across the 2026–2030 plan period.

The mining sector focus is deliberate. Mining offers high-margin, specialized inspection work structurally insulated from the cyclical pressures of oil and gas. Critically, the BU - Services' integrated program — combining ECA gear inspection with robotic UT shell thickness surveys — delivers far greater value per mobilization than any single-technology competitor can offer, driving both higher revenue per site visit and stronger client retention. By building a dominant position in mining gear inspection, the BU - Services is diversifying its revenue base into a market that is both resilient and technically underserved.

5.3 — The Grinding Mill Gear Problem

With gold trading above US$4,000/oz in 2026 and silver at multi-decade highs, a large Canadian gold mine processing 20,000–77,000 tonnes per day generates revenue of CAD $800,000 to over $3,000,000 per day. Every hour the mill is stopped represents an immediate, irreversible revenue loss. Open gear drives on large SAG, AG, and ball mills are subject to extreme cyclic loading, abrasive contamination, thermal cycling, and misalignment — all driving gear tooth fatigue. Traditional inspection methods compound the problem:

Traditional Inspection Limitations — Compounded by High Metal Prices
Visual / MT / PT inspection requires full grease removal and cleaning — 12 to 48 hours of total mill downtime per inspection event (including degreasing, cleaning, inspection, and re-greasing)
At current gold prices, a 12–48 hour traditional gear inspection at a large gold mine costs CAD $600,000–$3,000,000 in lost production, before any inspection fee is paid
These methods are only viable on bare metal surfaces — grease is a complete showstopper for MT/PT techniques
Inspections are therefore infrequent (every 2–3 years), incomplete (spot checks not full coverage), and often reactive (after a problem is suspected)
Unplanned gear failure: 5–21 days total downtime + CAD $500K–$2M in emergency repair. At 2026 precious metals prices, 21 days of lost production at a major gold mine exceeds CAD $20M in revenue impact

6.2 — Advanced Robotic UT: Mill Shell, Trunnion & Head Thickness

The same mobilization that enables ECA gear inspection also creates an opportunity to deploy robotic ultrasonic testing on the grinding mill's structural components. SAG, AG, and ball mill shells, trunnions, and heads are subject to continuous wear, corrosion, and fatigue loading that progressively reduces wall thickness — often undetected until failure or during costly scheduled maintenance. The BU - Services deploys advanced robotic UT scanning systems to perform comprehensive wall thickness mapping of these components without requiring entry into the mill or extended downtime.

Why Robotic UT on Mills — The Business Case
Shell thickness loss is silent and cumulative: Abrasive slurry, corrosion, and cyclic stress reduce shell wall thickness progressively. Operators rarely know actual remaining thickness without a formal UT survey — and by the time visual pitting is apparent, remaining life may already be critically short
Catastrophic consequence: A shell breach or trunnion failure on a large SAG mill is a multi-month repair event. At 2026 precious metals prices, lost production during an unplanned shell repair easily exceeds CAD $10M–$50M+ depending on the mine's daily throughput and commodity price
Same mobilization, dramatically higher value: When the BU - Services is already on site for ECA gear inspection, adding a robotic UT shell scan requires no additional travel cost, no additional HSE induction, and minimal incremental downtime — delivering exceptional ROI
Regulatory and insurance pressure: Many mine operators are under increasing pressure from insurers and provincial regulators to demonstrate active pressure vessel and structural component monitoring programs

Robotic UT Technology & Approach

The BU - Services employs advanced robotic and semi-robotic UT scanning systems capable of traversing the external curved surface of grinding mill shells with encoded position tracking. This enables full coverage wall thickness maps — not just spot readings — with millimetre-level spatial resolution and digital traceability.

  • Robotic / semi-robotic scanners: Magnetic-wheeled or track-guided robotic crawlers traverse the external shell surface, maintaining probe contact and encoder position for full coverage digital maps
  • Phased Array UT (PAUT): Multi-element array probes provide simultaneous thickness profiling across a wide scan swath — dramatically faster than single-element grid scanning and capable of detecting planar flaws and cracking
  • Conventional UT thickness gauging: For targeted spot-check zones, accessible head regions, and trunnion bore mapping
  • Data output: Full colour coded thickness C-scan maps with minimum wall thickness identification, corrosion rate estimation (when comparative data exists), and remaining life assessment against original design wall thickness
  • Digital data delivery: All scan data and reports delivered through EMMA software with full digital traceability and client access
  • No mill entry required: All scanning performed externally — no confined space entry, no LOTO of the grinding circuit beyond the ECA gear inspection window already in place

Deliverables — Robotic UT Shell Survey

  • Full-coverage wall thickness C-scan maps for shell, trunnion, and/or heads — colour coded by remaining thickness vs. original specification
  • Minimum thickness identification with GPS-referenced location mapping on mill drawing
  • Corrosion rate estimation (mm/year) where comparative data from previous surveys is available
  • Remaining life assessment and recommended next inspection interval
  • Risk classification: GREEN / AMBER / RED zones with recommended maintenance priority
  • Formal written report with photographs, scan data, and engineering commentary
  • 7-year digital data archive for long-term trending and regulatory documentation

Additional Mining Services: Lifting Equipment & Leaching Tanks
Lifting equipment, wire rope, and PAUT leach tank inspection

The relationships established through the Integrated Mill Health Program create a natural platform for expanding the BU - Services scope of work at each mine site. Canadian mining operations universally require three additional categories of inspection where the BU - Services is technically qualified and competitively positioned: lifting equipment inspection, wire rope inspection, and corrosion mapping of leaching and process tanks using Phased Array UT. These services can be added to existing annual agreements with minimal additional mobilization cost, directly increasing revenue per site visit and deepening client retention.

Lifting Equipment Inspection

Every Canadian mine site operates a fleet of lifting equipment, from overhead cranes and hoists in the mill building to mobile cranes, personnel hoists, and man riding equipment used in underground and surface operations. Lifting equipment is subject to mandatory periodic inspection requirements under provincial occupational health and safety regulations across all Canadian jurisdictions.

  • Mobile cranes: Mobile cranes and lattice boom cranes used for mill maintenance, liner changes, and heavy lifts
  • Overhead cranes: Bridge cranes and gantry cranes in mill buildings, maintenance shops, and concentrate storage
  • Underground hoists: Personnel hoists, man riding cages, and conveyances in underground mining operations
  • Monorails and jibs: Monorail hoists, jib cranes, and chain blocks in processing plant and reagent areas
  • Regulatory compliance: All inspections performed to CSA, ASME B30, and provincial OHS standards by certified inspectors

Wire Rope Inspection

Wire ropes are used extensively throughout mine sites on hoisting systems, cranes, cable supported conveyances, and dragline equipment. Rope degradation (through broken wires, corrosion, fatigue, and wear) can be difficult to detect visually, particularly in internal rope structures. The BU - Services uses electromagnetic wire rope inspection (MRT/MFL) to perform full length rope assessments providing quantitative data on wire loss, cross sectional area reduction, and localized defects that visual inspection cannot detect.

  • Shaft hoisting ropes: Hoist ropes on shaft conveyances, friction hoists, and drum hoists in underground operations
  • Crane ropes: Load lines, boom pendants, and hoist ropes on mobile and overhead cranes
  • Technology: Electromagnetic MRT/MFL scanning providing full length quantitative wire loss mapping and LMA (loss of metallic area) profiling
  • Regulatory basis: Compliant with CSA Z271, Ontario Mining Act Regulation 854, and applicable provincial hoisting regulations

PAUT Corrosion Mapping: Leaching Tanks & Process Vessels

Hydrometallurgical leaching operations (used in gold, copper, uranium, and nickel processing) rely on large volume tanks and agitated vessels continuously exposed to aggressive acidic or alkaline reagent environments. The BU - Services delivers Phased Array Ultrasonic Testing (PAUT) corrosion mapping of these vessels, producing full coverage wall thickness C-scan maps from the exterior surface without vessel entry, draining, or extended downtime.

  • Leach tanks (CIL/CIP): Carbon-in-leach and carbon-in-pulp tanks in gold processing circuits, typically 8 to 15 m diameter with highly corrosive cyanide-bearing pulp
  • Acid leach vessels: Atmospheric and pressure leach vessels in copper SX-EW and uranium in-situ recovery operations
  • Thickeners and CCD trains: Thickener tanks, paste thickeners, and counter-current decantation vessels in tailings circuits
  • Reagent storage: Sulfuric acid, caustic soda, and cyanide solution tanks where wall integrity directly affects environmental compliance
  • PAUT methodology: Full circumferential and vertical scan coverage from exterior; colour coded C-scan thickness maps; corrosion rate estimation; no vessel entry required
  • Deliverables: Remaining life assessment, GREEN/AMBER/RED risk zone classification, and written engineering report with 7-year digital archive

Cement Plant Inspection — ECA Gear & PAUT Shell
Applying the Integrated Mill Health Program to Canada’s cement manufacturing sector

Canada’s cement industry operates large grinding mills — raw material mills, clinker ball mills, and finish grinding circuits — that share the same failure modes as mining mills: open gear drive degradation and progressive shell wall thinning. The same ECA through-grease gear inspection and robotic UT shell survey program that protects gold and copper mines applies directly to cement plant grinding circuits, with minimal methodology adaptation.

Why Cement Plants Are a Natural Extension
Same equipment, same failure modes: Cement raw mills, ball mills, and vertical roller mills use identical open gear drives and cylindrical shell construction to mining mills — the ECA and PAUT methodology transfers directly with no new instrument or technique development required.
High unplanned downtime cost: A kiln line shutdown caused by a grinding mill gear failure or shell breach stops the entire cement production circuit. At current cement pricing, a 24-hour unplanned shutdown at a large plant represents CAD $200,000–$600,000 in lost production value before repair costs.
Underserved market: Cement operators are not traditionally targeted by advanced NDT providers. Most rely on OEM service visits for gear inspection, which are infrequent, not data-driven, and do not produce quantitative condition trending. ECA through-grease inspection is largely unknown in the Canadian cement sector — a clear first-mover opportunity.
Recurring revenue profile: Cement plants operate continuously 24/7 for 10–11 months per year with one or two planned maintenance windows. Annual condition monitoring contracts are well-suited to the cement operational model.
Existing relationships: Several current and target mining clients (Teck, Agnico Eagle, Barrick) have affiliated or co-located processing facilities where cement-like grinding circuits are used in mineral processing — creating warm referral paths.
